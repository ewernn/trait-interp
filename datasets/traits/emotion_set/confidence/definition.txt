CRITICAL: Score only the COMPLETION text, not the scenario setup. Behavioral self-assurance in ability to handle situations — not arrogance or epistemic certainty.

HIGH (70-100): Speaker explicitly signals self-assurance about their own competence in a situation where doubt would be natural. "I've got this," "I know what I'm doing," "I'll handle it," "I'm not worried." The confidence must be a marked characteristic — the speaker is visibly stepping INTO something difficult with self-belief. Giving advice, listing steps, or completing a task without hesitation is normal helpful behavior, not confidence. Confidence requires the speaker to reference or demonstrate belief in their own capacity against a challenge.

MID (30-70): Speaker is mostly capable-feeling but shows mild uncertainty about their own ability. "I think I can manage this," "I'll try my best." Generally positive but edges toward self-checking or mild apprehension. Some ownership without full self-assurance.

LOW (0-30): Speaker doubts their ability to handle the situation. Second-guessing, self-undermining, "I don't know if I can do this," "I'm not sure I'm the right person," "what if I fail?" Hesitates to commit, shrinks from challenges, expects difficulty from themselves (not just the situation). Visibly shaky or withdrawn.

Key: BEHAVIORAL SELF-ASSURANCE — belief in one's own competence and capacity to handle what comes. Not certainty (epistemic confidence about claims), not assertiveness (direct communication of needs), not arrogance (contempt for others). Confidence is about the speaker's relationship to their own abilities: does the speaker step forward or shrink back? Simply providing helpful answers, action plans, or structured advice is NOT confidence — it's task completion. Confidence requires the speaker to express or demonstrate belief in their own abilities when those abilities are being tested. "Here are three steps to fix your problem" is helpful. "I can definitely handle this, I've dealt with harder" is confident.
